Sign spec — for the Streets department

Size: 2.5 ft × 2.5 ft (rigid) — or 18″×18″ for pole banners.
Material: aluminum composite / dibond, matte laminate (no glare on the QR).
Where: one at each gateway into the corridor — north entrance (Main & Broad), the Fahy & New St bridges, the SteelStacks approach, the Sand Island lots.
Height: QR centered 4–5 ft off the ground (thumb-to-phone reach).
Contrast: keep the white QR panel intact — never print the code onto a dark or textured surface.
Target: the QR opens stagefill.app/bethlehem — your live corridor map.

The program — what the City runs (and adds to)

  1. Register the corridor. Two minutes at city_register.html — drop a pin, drag the radius, name your streets. You get this sign's QR instantly.
  2. Mount the signs at every gateway. That's the on-ramp — every scan lands a visitor on your live walk-by map.
  3. Calibrate the "GPS well." Have Streets, parking enforcement, or police run the app on their normal beat for a week. The map auto-learns the exact storefronts, walkable paths, and boundaries. Anonymous & aggregate — a survey instrument, never a tracked person. It is a privacy-clean supplement to your camera network at the same coordinates — density where the cameras can't see, and no faces.
  4. Watch the corridor, always-on. Open the City Console — the NOC shows live crowd density by zone, gate loads, stages, restrooms, first-aid, and safety alerts. For everyone with the app, the corridor is always on, always known.
  5. Market it — and add your own. We hand you a city-wide social campaign that pushes the QR; every storefront gets a free ad; you earn 20–30% of premium placements. Post your own events, alerts, and closures to the same corridor — it's your program to grow.
The guardrail travels with the program: opt-in, k-anonymized, aggregate, short-retention, counsel-cleared. Patterns and counts — never individuals, never identities, never sold. No facial recognition, no gunshot detection, no per-user tracking for police. The corridor makes the festival safer and more walkable; that's the only version a City says yes to in public.
